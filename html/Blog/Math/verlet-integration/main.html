Just here to prove it to myself while noodling around with some code.

Recall the definition of a taylor series expansion with the normal caveats of smoothness etc. (Also, check out Madhava, Jason Gregory and Newton for historical development; it's also amusing to think about the sheer volume of Stigler's law occurences throughout history; its also heady and humbling to consider that this was used to calculate Halley's Comet trajectories initially and now it's a back of a napkin exercise )

\[ \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{f^{n}(a)}{n!} \left( t - a \right)^n \]

Where \[ a \] is the point around which we're expanding and \[ t \] is the variable we're creating the expansion for.

For a simulation, with a discrete time step associated with the physics tick/ cycle, $ Δ{t} $, we need to be careful about/ decide what and where we're expanding.

We want to know what happens at the next time step: $ x\left( t + Δ{t} \right) $ (This means we're creating an expansion for a function that can talk about that point.)

We know what is happening at the current step, so our expansion is expanding around that point: $ a = t $

Subsituting into the Taylor series formula:

\[ \implies \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{f^{n}(t)}{n!} \left( \Delta{t} \right)^n \]

This is if it's a forward time step, notice if it's a backward time step, all that would change is that we'd be exanding for $ x\left( t - Δ{t} \right) $

\[ \implies \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{f^{n}(t)}{n!} \left( -\Delta{t} \right)^n \]

The trick or method is to sum (simple verlet) or take the difference (stoermer-verlet) of the two.

Summing allows the even powers in the expansion to cancel which gives the method its stability (\(E \propto O^4\)).

\[ \implies x\left( t + \Delta{t} \right) + x\left( t - \Delta{t} \right) = 2x\left( t \right) - xleft( t - \Delta{t} \right) + a left( t \right) {left( \Delta{t} \right)}^2 \]

To talk about the next time step, we need only to know about the current position, the last position and the current acceleration resolution.
